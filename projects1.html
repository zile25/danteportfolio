Project 1: Introduction to 3D Printing

Return to Main Menu

Print 1: Nametag

I was pleasantly surprised by how easy it was to get started with 3D printing. After downloading Prusa Slicer, I imported the .3mf file, added my name, and positioned the text in the center. My first print failed due to a loose X-axis motor, causing layer shifting and over-extrusion. Once fixed, I reprinted using a color change, and the result was much better. I learned how to properly load filament—requiring firm pressure without snapping the brittle PLA. Aside from minor stringing and blobbing on the logo, the final print came out great. A heat gun easily removes those stray strings.

Print 2: Calibration / Torture Print

I printed the 3D Benchy test model to evaluate print accuracy. The result showed slight under-extrusion and blobbing, but the overall quality was solid. Measuring six distances ranging from 5–50 mm, I was within 1–2 mm of the benchmark values. This test increased my confidence in the Prusa printer’s reliability. I also learned that .STL files store only 3D geometry, while .3MF files include printer settings, colors, and supports — making them my preferred format going forward.

Print 3: Open-Source Assistive Device

I printed a 3-way drink opener, designed by someone whose mother has Cushing’s Disease. The round toothed sections help users with weak grip strength open bottle caps with less force, while the other ends function as a can opener and traditional bottle opener. The print was smooth and the tool worked well in all tests. Although PLA may lack long-term durability, a stronger material could address this. The documentation was clear and included assembly tips. Overall, this project demonstrated how 3D printing can create simple yet impactful assistive devices.
