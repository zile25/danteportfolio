Part 1: Experimenting with Storage

As we started this project I had a few ideas for problems to solve with our newly acquired skills in design and manufacturing. But one great use for 3d printing I see lots of in our classroom and the makerlab is storage. As you see in the 2 images above, my first print was two interlocking cylinders that could be used as an aesthetically pleasing storage container. As I learned, when printing hollow objects they need to be set to vase mode to tell the slicer not to add automatic supports inside the object and because I hadn’t done this the two prints had grid structures inside and were unable to hold anything. I thought of reprinting these but was pushed in another direction when my professor mentioned Gridfinity, the open-source CAD website specializing in storage designs. This is where I designed my final products for this project.

Part 2: My Plier Storage Solution

Now armed with Gridfinity I identified my area of the classroom to improve as the pliers drawer. The pliers draw is one of many in the front of the room and has plenty of space for the 20-25 pairs of pliers we have but it is visually nicer to have separate organizers for each size plier (we have 4 different sizes) and stop them from clicking into each other when being taken out and put back. I used a digital caliper to measure the dimensions of the 4 different sets of pliers and these measurements can be found on my printables posting under each model I specify the dimensions and how many of each item can be stored. Using these measurements I started creating boxes in Gridfinity. First I made the length and height the right size for each plier then multiplied the thickness by the # to fit in each box which was 11 for the largest box and 6 for the other 3. I then inserted the correct number of walls (5 or 10) and evenly spaced them out so each plier can fit correctly. Now with my completed designs I exported into .stl files and imported into PrusaSlicer. I used our normal print settings and added supports “everywhere” which only added them to the bottom. The details in print settings and the supports on the bottom of my blue box can be seen in the images above, these did a good job and aside from normal imperfections the prints came out very well, the supports did a good job of ensuring the grip pattern on the bottom came out nicely, and I love the look of the silk PLA filament used for the prints.

Part 3: Reflection and Sharing My Models

In conclusion I really enjoyed creating a storage solution that will continue to be a part of the engineering classroom and we can see my three prints holding many pairs of pliers and fitting well in the drawer in the images above. I also posted my .stl files and dimensions for each on Printables which can be accessed using the button at the top of the page, unfortunately I ran out of class time to print the largest box which was just over the 8 inch length limit for our Prusa Mini’s and needed to be printed in a Raised3D and sliced in IdeaMaker but I may print it out during some downtime in class. One or two of those boxes would finish the set and could organize all of our class pliers. Overall I’m very happy with the results of this project and am exc ited to have added Gridfinity as a CAD software in my toolbox, one feature I chose to exclude was printing a grid layer to place the boxes into. I chose to leave this out as in class the boxes will be placed into drawers anyway and I needed to use different grid sizes to get the correct dimensions for the different sized pliers. Overall I’m glad for the suggestion to organize a random drawer and Gridfinity and our printers were the right tool for the job. A final touch I made was to to back and improve my website's design especially for the Project 2 page after recieveing your feedback on what worked and what could be improved.
